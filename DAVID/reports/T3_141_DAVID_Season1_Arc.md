## Season 1 Premise

**DAVID · The Archive** is a documentary-host series asking one question per episode: *What did they actually say — and how do we prove it?*

Season 1 is a 12-episode arc built on the dead-language corpus slate. Each episode stands alone, but together they move through a deliberate argument about how language dies, survives, and gets recovered — starting with the most accessible entry points (Latin, Greek) and deepening toward the strangest and most honest cases (Sumerian, Old Church Slavonic).

**Season 1 thesis:** *Dead languages don't disappear — they leave traces. This season follows DAVID as he reads the traces.*

---

## Season Structure

Season 1 divides into three acts of four episodes each. The act structure is invisible to the viewer — each episode is complete and self-contained — but the thematic progression builds the Archive's character and demonstrates its methodology across expanding time and geography.

---

## Act I — The Western Canon (Eps 1–4)
*The Archive opens with the languages that most viewers have heard of — but have never actually heard.*

These four episodes establish the Archive's voice and methodology. They answer the implicit viewer doubt: "I thought Latin was dead — what do you mean it still matters?" They move from the familiar (Latin is still read everywhere) to the less familiar (Ancient Greek has a pitch accent nobody uses) to the remote (Old English sounds almost alien) to the dramatic (Old Norse is half attested, half reconstructed).

| Ep | Slug | Title | Arc Function |
|----|------|-------|-------------|
| 1 | `david_latin_corpus_v1` | Why Latin Never Really Died | **Series opener.** Establishes the Archive formula: attested vs reconstructed; corpus-first; no overclaiming. Answers "why does this matter?" with a live argument: priests, jurists, scientists still read it aloud. |
| 2 | `david_ancient_greek_corpus_v1` | Restoring the Pitch Accent | **Deepens the method.** Homer has been read for three millennia — almost never with the pitch accent the poets built into the meter. The Archive can put it back. Viewer discovers: even "well-known" dead languages still have lost dimensions. |
| 3 | `david_old_english_corpus_v1` | Beowulf's Tongue in Manuscript | **The English surprise.** The language of Beowulf and Alfred is the ancestor of Modern English — and sounds like nothing the viewer knows. Establishes: corpus-first means reading the actual manuscript, not the textbook summary. |
| 4 | `david_old_norse_corpus_v1` | Runes Attested, Sagas Reconstructed | **The honest complexity beat.** Not all evidence is equal. Carved runestones are directly attested; skaldic meter on vellum is reconstructed. The Archive names the difference on screen, every time. Sets up the dual-label convention for the season. |

---

## Act II — Deeper Time, Wider World (Eps 5–8)
*The Archive goes further back and further afield. The evidence gets stranger; the methodology holds.*

Act II moves from the medieval/classical world into older and more geographically diverse languages. Gothic proves a single book can preserve a whole language. Sumerian is the deepest time the corpus currently reaches. Sanskrit inverts the usual story (memory, not ink). Hebrew is the revival success story.

| Ep | Slug | Title | Arc Function |
|----|------|-------|-------------|
| 5 | `david_gothic_corpus_v1` | A Language Saved by One Bible | **The single-witness episode.** Gothic exists because Wulfila translated scripture in the fourth century. One text. The Archive asks: what can we reconstruct from a single source? Companion to Episode 1's "many sources" — the contrast is the argument. |
| 6 | `david_sumerian_corpus_v1` | The First Written Language | **The deepest-time episode.** Not Indo-European, not Semitic, not related to anything we know — and the oldest language in the slate by two millennia. Closes Act I/II divide with the reminder: the Archive doesn't require familiarity, only corpus. |
| 7 | `david_sanskrit_corpus_v1` | The Language Memory Kept Alive | **Oral-tradition counterpoint.** Latin was preserved in ink. Sanskrit was preserved in memory — recited so carefully it can be heard near its origin. Pāṇini's grammar (c. 400 BCE) is one of the most precise linguistic documents ever produced. Challenges the viewer's assumption that "written = preserved." |
| 8 | `david_biblical_hebrew_corpus_v1` | The Language That Came Back | **The revival episode.** Stopped being spoken at home for centuries; kept in worship; revived as a living vernacular in the 20th century. The Archive's only entry where corpus-study contributed directly to a modern revival. Emotional anchor of Act II. |

---

## Act III — Beyond the Expected Alphabet (Eps 9–12)
*The Archive reaches languages the viewer has probably never considered — and finds the methodology holds, even at the edge of what the evidence can prove.*

Act III is the season's hardest, most rewarding run. Akkadian pairs with Sumerian as a cuneiform deep-time bridge. Middle Egyptian is the honest case where we have the writing system but lost the vowels for millennia. Nahuatl opens the New World — the only language in Season 1 with living descendants who speak a closely related variety. Old Church Slavonic closes the season with a mirror image of Episode 1's Latin: scripture-as-preservation, across a different half of the ancient world.

| Ep | Slug | Title | Arc Function |
|----|------|-------|-------------|
| 9 | `david_akkadian_corpus_v1` | The Voice Inside the Clay | **Cuneiform bridge.** Babylon and Assyria's tongue, written in the same clay medium as Sumerian — and forgotten so completely that 19th-century travellers thought the writing was decorative. The Epic of Gilgamesh is the corpus anchor. Pairs with Episode 6 (Sumerian) as the cuneiform double. |
| 10 | `david_middle_egyptian_corpus_v1` | The Sound Behind the Hieroglyphs | **The honest hard case.** Hieroglyphics we read with confidence — and whose vowels the script almost never wrote. The Archive names the gap: consonants ATTESTED, vowels RECONSTRUCTED via Coptic. Sets up Season 2's Coptic episode as the "vowel revelation" sequel. |
| 11 | `david_classical_nahuatl_corpus_v1` | The Tongue the Conquest Wrote Down | **The New World episode.** Aztec language written down by conquered and conquerors alike in the 16th century — and descendants still speak a closely related variety today. The Archive's first episode where living descendants are part of the story. Opens Season 1 toward the living present. |
| 12 | `david_old_church_slavonic_corpus_v1` | The Bible That Built an Alphabet | **Season finale.** The first written Slavic language, invented in the ninth century so scripture could be read. Cyril and Methodius built the alphabet — then translated into it — in a single generation. Mirrors Episode 1 (Latin, scripture-as-preservation) and Episode 5 (Gothic, single-bible survival) as the season's closing argument: *literacy itself was the act of rescue.* |

## Season 1 Thematic Arc (Condensed)

```
Ep 1–2: The Archive opens. Method established. Familiar languages made strange.
Ep 3–4: Medieval north. Manuscript vs. reconstruction. Dual-label convention set.
Ep 5–6: One bible. The deepest clay. Evidence at the edge of memory.
Ep 7–8: Memory vs. ink. Revival possible. The corpus is not always paper.
Ep 9–10: Cuneiform double. The honest limit. Vowels the script never wrote.
Ep 11–12: Living descendants. The final rescue. Literacy as an act of preservation.
```
