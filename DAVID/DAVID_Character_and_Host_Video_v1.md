# DAVID — Character Design & Host-Format Video (v1)

**2026-06-18 · Owner: NEXUS (DAVID lane).** DAVID as the channel's recurring on-screen host — the recognizable face audiences follow — paired with language-native avatars for the "hear it spoken" moment. All synthetic, honest-by-design.

---

## Who DAVID is
The brand's namesake linguist — **the keeper of the Archive.** Obsessive, patient, corpus-first (the Prometheus protocol). His whole reason for being is the core question: ***"What did they actually say — and how do we prove it?"*** He brings dead languages back to *audible* life and always shows his work. He is the trust signal: calm, rigorous, never hype.

## What he looks like
- **Age/face:** reads ~45–55. Dark hair flecked with silver, neatly kept. A lined, intelligent face; calm, attentive eyes that warm when something delights him. Conveys patience + authority — someone who has spent decades with these texts.
- **Wardrobe:** timeless and understated — soft dark fabric (charcoal or deep navy), fine texture, maybe one worn detail (a leather strap, reading glasses pushed up into his hair). **Deliberately NOT period costume** — DAVID is the *modern bridge* to the ancient world, so he reads as "now" surrounded by "then." (Era dress is reserved for the language-native avatars.)
- **Presence:** still, grounded, leans in when it matters. Hands occasionally gesture toward an open codex or "trace" a glyph in the air.

## The set — "The Archive" (recurring, = brand recognition)
A deep study/library in warm pools of lamplight: shelves of manuscripts, scrolls, and clay tablets from many cultures receding into soft shadow. A long worktable holds an open codex, a clay tablet, a scroll, and one recurring brass lamp. **Same set every episode** — the instant-recognition anchor, like a late-night host's desk.

## Voice & how he acts
- **Voice:** measured, resonant, unhurried; mid-low register; precise diction. Documentary gravitas (Attenborough-calm) with a spark of genuine delight. Accessible, never stuffy.
- **Signature beat:** poses the core question — *"What did they actually say?"* — beat — *"…and how do we prove it?"* Recurring tagline that becomes the channel's identity.
- **Honest-by-design, on camera:** always says *and* shows whether something is **attested** or **reconstructed**. Treats uncertainty as part of the wonder, not a weakness: *"We can't be certain — but the evidence points here."*
- **Wonder under composure:** *"No one has spoken this sentence aloud in fifteen hundred years. Listen."*
- **Never** clickbait in delivery (titles can be punchy; DAVID himself stays trustworthy). The honesty *is* the brand moat.

## Format — the hybrid (recommended)
1. **DAVID hosts** in the Archive (consistent face/set) → sets up the language + the question.
2. **Cut to the language-native avatar** (Roman scholar, Viking scribe, etc.) to actually *hear the line* spoken in its world — the immersive payoff.
3. **Back to DAVID** to teach the key sound shift + show the provenance card.
Best of both worlds: a host people follow + per-language immersion.

*(PIE is fully reconstructed — the boldest honesty test, which is exactly why it's a strong opener. Labels lean hard on "reconstructed.")* ~42s.

*Caption: DAVID · The Archive · 🔶 reconstructed*

**6–11s · the question (push-in on DAVID):**
> "What did they actually say? And how do we prove it?"

**11–26s · the reconstruction (DAVID voices it, clearly labeled):**
DAVID speaks a reconstructed PIE phrase, slowly. On-screen: the asterisk-form text + IPA + a bold **RECONSTRUCTED — not attested** tag. (Asterisk on every PIE form, per scholarly convention.)

**26–36s · DAVID explains the method (back in Archive, gesturing to texts):**
> "We compare its daughters — Latin, Sanskrit, Old English — and work backward to the sound that *must* have stood behind them all. That's the comparative method. It's reconstruction, not a recording — and we'll always tell you which is which."

**36–42s · provenance card + tagline (DAVID VO):**
> "Bring the dead tongues back — one attestation at a time. DAVID."
*Provenance card: sources (Wikipedia/Wiktionary CC BY-SA, Wikidata CC0), retrieved date, RECONSTRUCTED label.*
