Purpose: Training dataset source material for law-enforcement forensic document

Compiled: 2026-06-23

SECTION 1: CHECK FRAUD — MICR TAMPERING, CHEMICAL ALTERATION, CHECK WASHING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

LE forensic document examiners treat altered checks under SWGDOC E03-13 / ANSI/ASB
Standard 035. Common fraud vectors: (1) check washing — solvent removal of payee
and amount ink (acetone, toluene, bleach solutions) while MICR E-13B magnetic ink
at the document bottom often survives; (2) chemical alteration of payee/amount fields
via oxidizing agents, correction fluid, or re-inking over bleached areas; (3) MICR
line tampering — re-encoding routing/transit/account numbers with magnetic ink pens,
strip-and-replace of MICR toner bands, or photocopied MICR lacking proper magnetic
signal.

Detection (non-destructive first, SWGDOC E03 §7.4): oblique and transmitted light for
fiber disturbance, discoloration, and smearing in courtesy/legal amount lines. VSC
multispectral imaging (UV-A 365 nm, visible, IR 780–850 nm) separates ink batches;
washed zones show fluorescence anomalies or absent IR luminescence. MSFI or MICR
reader verifies signal strength and character spacing per ANSI X9; tampered MICR shows
hand-drawn strokes, inconsistent flux density, or registration misalignment. Washing
leaves roughened paper, disturbed sizing, and ESDA-detectable fiber disruption.
Stroke intersection order proves post-issuance alteration. Destructive ink comparison
(SWGDOC M01/M02) requires submitter consultation (E03 §7.13). See ANSI/ASB 172 for
mechanical checkwriter impressions.

SECTION 2: SIGNATURE FORGERY — METHODS AND HANDWRITING IDENTIFICATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Signature examination follows SWGDOC E01-13 / ANSI/ASB Standard 070 (Examination
of Handwritten Items), applying ACE-V: Analysis of discriminating elements,
Comparison, Evaluation, optional Verification. Forgery categories: (1) freehand
simulation — forger copies a model signature without mechanical aid, producing slow,
tremulous line quality, pen lifts, retouching, and inconsistent letter formations;
(2) tracing — transmitted light, carbon intermediates, or indentations reveal guide
marks; ESDA may recover underlying model signature impressions; (3) auto-pen /
autopen / robotic signature devices — reproduce uniform pressure, identical
waveforms across multiple specimens, and absence of natural range of variation.

Examiners evaluate pen pressure (indentation depth via oblique light and ESDA),
writing speed (line width variation, tapered terminals, ink feathering), and tremor
(unnatural high-frequency oscillation in curves, patched strokes). SWGDOC E01
§7.12.4 mandates assessment of line quality, freedom of execution, hesitations,
stops, patching, and tremor against known (K) standards. Simulated signatures lack
internal consistency with the writer's range of variation in speed, pressure, and
proportion. Blind simulations may show model dependence (microscopic similarities
to the exemplar). Conclusions use SWGDOC Terminology for Expressing Conclusions
(identification, elimination, inconclusive, or insufficient evidence). Original
documents required; photocopies may lack sufficient stroke detail (E01 §7.5).
Graphology is explicitly excluded from FDE scope per SWGDOC guidance.

SECTION 3: DOCUMENT ALTERATION — OBLITERATION, INSERTION, PAGE SUBSTITUTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SWGDOC E03-13 defines alteration as modification by physical, chemical, or
mechanical means including obliterations, additions, overwritings, or erasures.
Obliteration methods: abrasive erasure (rubber/vinyl eraser leaving fiber nap and
surface sheen), chemical solvent erasure, opaque covering (correction tape/fluid,
acrylic paint), and digital cut-and-paste reprinting. Detection: side lighting
reveals raised/depressed erasure zones; transmitted light shows thin spots; UV/IRL
imaging may recover luminescent ink beneath opaque covers; EDD (electrostatic
detection device per ANSI/ASB 044) visualizes fiber disturbances and prior
indentations of obliterated text.

Insertion detection: crowded or awkward placement of text (E03 §7.3.3), multiple
writing instruments, inconsistent slant/size, and intersection sequence analysis —
altered strokes crossing original lines indicate later application. Digital
insertions show font inconsistency, halftone dot patterns, and edge artifacts under
high magnification. Page substitution: physical match examination (SWGDOC Physical
Match standard) compares tear/cut/perforation edges; paper stock comparison (SWGDOC
M03 non-destructive paper examination) evaluates basis weight, color, fluorescent
optical brighteners, and watermark alignment; staple hole patterns, fold lines, and
binding marks (E03 §7.3.12) must align across purportedly contiguous pages. ESDA
indentation mapping can link a substituted page to a different writing pad. Sequence
determination (which ink crossed which) is critical for proving fraudulent revision.

SECTION 4: PHYSICAL EXAMINATION TOOLS — ESDA, VSC, INFRARED, ULTRAVIOLET
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ESDA (Electrostatic Detection Apparatus, Foster+Freeman ESDA2): non-destructive
indentation visualization per ANSI/ASB 044. Procedure: humidify document (RH <60%),
place on grounded platen under Mylar charging film, corona charge (~7 kV), develop
with cascade toner beads. Detects indentations several sheets deep and decades old.
Fitness-for-use (FFU) control required each run. Run BEFORE ninhydrin or wet
chemical processing (E03 §5.3). Also reveals mechanical erasures and footwear
impressions on paper.

VSC (Video Spectral Comparator): forensic-grade multispectral document examination
system. Incident illumination: UV-A (365 nm), UV-B (308 nm), UV-C (255 nm), visible,
IR (780–1100 nm), coaxial, oblique, and transmitted modes. Camera spectral range
350–1100 nm with narrowband LED arrays (1000+ waveband combinations). Applications:
ink differentiation via IR reflectance and infrared luminescence (IRL); recovery of
charred/soot-covered entries; yellow enhancement for MIC/CPS codes; side-by-side
subtractive comparison of stroke images. PVMS microspectrometer accessory (400–850 nm,
5 nm resolution) identifies ink spectral curves.

Infrared examination: IR-absorbing inks become transparent under IR illumination,
revealing underlying writing (E03 §6.3.1 RIR/IRL). UV examination: long-wave UV-A
excites fluorescent security features and distinguishes ink batches by fluorescence
pattern (SWGDOC M01). Short-wave UV-C detects paper bleaching and erasure zones.
All observations documented per SWGDOC Image Capture and Storage Technology standard.

SECTION 5: STANDARDS — ASTM E2290, SWGDOC, AND ASB SUCCESSOR DOCUMENTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ASTM E2290-07a (Standard Guide for Examination of Handwritten Items, Committee E30)
was withdrawn January 2016 with no ASTM replacement; content migrated to SWGDOC and
subsequently to ANSI/ASB Standards Board (AAFS) forensic document examination
Consensus Body. Current authoritative documents (per swgdoc.org, 2021):

  ANSI/ASB 070 — Examination of Handwritten Items (supersedes SWGDOC E01-13, ASTM E2290)
  ANSI/ASB 035 — Examination of Documents for Alterations (supersedes SWGDOC E03-13)
  ANSI/ASB 044 — Examination of Documents for Indentations (supersedes SWGDOC E02-13)
  ANSI/ASB 011 — Scope of Expertise in Forensic Document Examination
  ANSI/ASB 155 — Minimum Training Requirements (≥24 months supervised apprenticeship)
  ANSI/ASB 172 — Mechanical Checkwriter Impressions and Machines
  SWGDOC M01/M02 — Forensic Writing Ink Comparison and Identification (still active)
  SWGDOC Terminology documents — conclusion language and QDE definitions

SWGDOC (Scientific Working Group for Forensic Document Examination, est. 1997)
develops consensus standards for federal, state, and local LE document laboratories.
Training minimums reference ASTM E2388-11 / SWGDOC G02-13 (now ASB 155). Examiners
apply Huber & Headrick identification principles and document all procedures,
limitations, and conclusions. Chemical/destructive methods require submitter
consultation. FDE scope explicitly excludes graphology per SWGDOC policy statement.

SECTION 6: FAMOUS LE CASES — QUESTIONED DOCUMENT EXAMINATION AS PIVOTAL EVIDENCE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Lindbergh kidnapping (1934): Albert S. Osborn compared ransom note handwriting to
Bruno Hauptmann's exemplars; stroke comparison and ink/paper analysis were central
to conviction. Established FDE expert testimony precedent in U.S. courts.

Alger Hiss perjury trial (1950s): Martin Tytell demonstrated "fake typewriter"
hypothesis — Woodstock typewriter forgery to replicate Hiss's known machine
characteristics; typewriter fracture patterns and typeface identification pivotal.

West Midlands Serious Crime Squad (UK, 1980s–1990s): ESDA indentations on police
interview notes proved witness statements were altered, signed on blank pages, or
written in reverse sequence. ESDA evidence contributed to 60+ quashed convictions
and squad disbandment — landmark use of indentation evidence in LE misconduct cases.

Mark Hofmann forgery/murder (1980–84): FDE exposed Salamander Letter forgeries among
purported Mormon historical documents; ink age, paper anachronisms, and
handwriting simulation analysis proved fraud.

Killian memos (2004): CBS 60 Minutes Bush National Guard documents; typographic
analysis (proportional spacing, superscript th), toner vs. typewriter technology
timeline, and font identification (Times New Roman vs. period typewriters) led to
document authentication failure and Rather resignation.

Anthrax letters (2001): FBI FDE and materials analysis examined block-letter
handprinting, paper stock, ink, and indentation patterns; questioned document
examination integrated with microbial forensics in domestic terrorism investigation.

SECTION 7: CHAIN OF CUSTODY — QUESTIONED DOCUMENTS AS PHYSICAL EVIDENCE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Questioned documents admitted as physical evidence require unbroken chain of custody
documenting every transfer from seizure to courtroom. LE agencies typically follow
agency policy aligned with ASTM E1459 (Standard Guide for Physical Evidence
Handling) and NFPA 921 evidence-management principles. Required elements per
transfer: case number, item description (unique evidence ID), date/time, from/to
custodian signatures, purpose of transfer (examination, storage, court), and
condition notes.

FDE-specific handling per SWGDOC E03 §5.3 and E01 §5.3: questioned documents
examined BEFORE latent print chemical processing (ninhydrin, DFO, cyanoacrylate
fuming) to preserve indentations and ink fluorescence. Handlers wear clean cotton or
nitrile gloves; handle by edges; avoid plastic sleeves that trap moisture. Store flat
in breathable paper envelopes, not PVC; control humidity to preserve ESDA-viable
indentations. Non-destructive examinations precede destructive ink/solvent tests;
submitter authorization required for destructive methods (E03 §7.13.2).

Documentation: intake log, examination worksheet, photographic/image capture per
SWGDOC Image Capture standard (metadata embedded: examiner, date, instrument, lighting
wavelength). Evidence return/seal integrity verified on return to evidence vault.
Breaks in chain of custody create admissibility challenges under Daubert/Frye;
examiner testimony must establish continuous custody and integrity of the specific
item examined. Digital working copies require hash verification against original
scans; originals remain authoritative for stroke-level comparison.
